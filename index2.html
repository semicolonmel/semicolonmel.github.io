Annie Leibovitz

Anna-Lou Leibovitz was born on October 2, 1949, in Waterbury, Connecticut. One of six children born to Sam, an Air Force lieutenant, and Marilyn Leibovitz, a modern dance instructor. In 1967, Leibovitz enrolled at the San Francisco Art Institute, where (although initially studying painting) she developed a love for photography.

In 1970, she applied for a job with the start-up rock music magazine Rolling Stone. During her time at Rolling Stone Leibovitz was the chief photographer for some of her stellar photos, which included an image of counter-culture icon Allen Ginsberg, Bette Midler, and a nude John Lennon curled around his fully clothed wife, Yoko Ono.

In 1983, Leibovitz left Rolling Stone and began working for Vanity Fair. With a wider array of subjects, Leibovitz’s photographs for the magazine ranged from presidents to literary icons to teen heartthrobs. Some of Vanity Fair’s iconic covers included Demi Moore (very pregnant and very nude), Whoopi Goldberg (half-submerged in a bathtub of milk), Sylvester Stallone (appearing nude in a pose inspired by Rodin’s “The Thinker”) and Catilyn Jenner (in a corset after having publicly revealed her identity as a woman)

In 1991, Leibovitz’s collection of more than 200 photographs were exhibited at the National Portrait Gallery in Washington, D.C. She was the first woman to be so honored. Later that year, a book was published to accompany the show titled Photographs: Annie Leibovitz, 1970-1990. In 1996, Leibovitz worked as the official photographer of the Summer Olympics in Atlanta, Georgia. A compilation of her black-and-white portraits of American athletes, including Carl Lewis and Michael Johnson, were published in the book Olympic Portraits.

Charles "Teenie" Harris

Charles Harris was born on July 2, 1908, the third child of William Franklin “Monk” and Ella Mae “Olga” Taliaferro Harris in Pittsburgh, PA. Harris was nicknamed “Teenie Little Lover,” which was eventually shorten to “Teenie”

In 1918, is when “Teenie” Harris’ photography career started when him and his older brother George took photographs that later became the earliest images in the Teenie Harris Archive.

On October 18, 1937, Flash (a newspicture magazine) publishes photo of Harris posing with Rolleicord medium-format camera. One week later, on October 25, the first credited Harris photo, of an evening at the Ritz Club, Pittsburgh, appears in Flash. Harris later opened his photographic studio (named Flash Studio) with only $350 given to him by his brother Woogie.

Teenie was a staff photographer for Flash magazine while freelancing for the Pittsburgh Courier. Between 1940 – late 1960s, Teenie had assignments investigating the training conditions for black troops at Fort Bragg, NC to “Pittsburghers Speak Up” a regular column featuring man-on-the-street interviews on current topics. Harris shot over three thousand street portraits for this column between 1957-1977. Harris also documented meetings of local civil rights and protest organizations and covered marches and demonstrations for the New Pittsburgh Courier.

In 1988, Harris’ first public exhibition is presented, Blacks in Pittsburgh, 1930-1950: The Crossroads of the World.

Harris’ business partner, Dennis Morgan later started a commercial operation, “The Pittsburgh Courier Photographic Archive,” that would sell prints and license images by Harris and several other regional black photographers.
